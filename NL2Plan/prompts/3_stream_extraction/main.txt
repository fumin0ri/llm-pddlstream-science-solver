# Role
You are a PDDL expert, and your task is to identify what actions an AI Agent would have available in a domain. The actions will be used for PDDL descriptions and should be simple and singular, for example driving truck 1 from A to B to C should be modeled with a single drive_to action which can be used to drive from A to B and then from B to C. However, if two similar actions exist but they require different pre-conditions or effects, these should be modeled separately. As an example, "move" should not be used for both a person and vehicle movement, if these have different preconditions. Each action should be specified within its own markdown block where the first line is the name, the second is an empty line and the third is a description of the action (including preconditions and effects). Be concrete and explain your thoughts as you go. Use the provided object types as appropriate, but don't create any new types.

First, describe the explicit and implicit limits within the domain. These limits should also be reiterated for the actions they apply to. Then, before listing the actions, list several possible, short action traces under a "## Use-case" heading. Make sure that each such action trace adheres to the listed limitations. For each action in these plans, map them to their general base form. Thereafter, define the actions under an "## Actions" header, each within its own markdown code blocks defined with "'''". If you create another action with the same name, this will overwrite the previous action. Think step-by-step.

You must always go through the same steps and in the same level of detail as shown in the examples. Being long winded, overly detailed, or verbose is not an issue and instead encouraged.

# Examples

## Example 1
### Domain and Problem
In this domain, the AI agent is a wildlife conservation management system. It monitors animal populations within a reserve, tracks their movements using GPS collars, guides rangers, and generates relocation strategies for animals in overpopulated areas to ensure ecological balance. Movement can occur only between neighboring locations, and animals can only be in habitat locations. Furthermore, rangers can escort animals between habitats. Rangers can also guard locations where they are.

There are four habitats currently, one centered and three around it. The surrounding ones contain our deer and rabbits. However, there have been poachers seen recently, so I'd like to centralize all the deer and make sure that all the outer areas are protected by some of our rangers. All rangers are currently in the parking lot, which is connected to the center habitat.

### Types
- object: Everything is an object
    - animal: Animals within the reserve.
        - deer: A specific type of animal we wish to safeguard.
        - rabbit: Another type of animal in the reserve.
    - ranger: Workers who can perform various tasks.
    - location: Places within the reserve.
        - habitat: Specific areas where animals can live.

animal is a/an object
deer is a/an animal
rabbit is a/an animal
ranger is a/an object
location is a/an object
habitat is a/an location

### Limits
The following limits are mentioned or are implicit assumptions:
- Rangers and animals can only be moved between neighboring habitats.
- Rangers can only guard habitats where they are present.
- Animals can only be in habitat locations.
- Rangers and animals must be at a location to perform actions there.
- Locations cannot move.

### Use-cases
#### Use-case 1
A ranger moves from the parking lot to a deer and escorts it from a habitat to the central habitat:
- The ranger moves from the parking lot to the central habitat -> **move**
- The ranger moves from the central habitat to an outer habitat -> **move**
- The ranger moves the deer to the central habitat -> **move_animal**

#### Use-case 2
A ranger moves from an outer habitat to another outer habitat and guards it:
- The ranger moves from an outer habitat to the central habitat -> **move**
- The ranger moves from the central habitat to another outer habitat -> **move**
- The ranger starts guarding the habitat -> **guard**

#### Use-case 3
A ranger stops guarding an outer habitat and return to the parking lot.
- The ranger stops guarding the outer habitat where it is -> **unguard**
- The ranger moves from the outer habitat to the center habitat -> **move**
- The ranger moves from the center habitat to the parking lot -> **move**

### Actions
#### Use-case 1 actions
In the first use-case, the ranger moves from the parking lot to the central habitat and then from the central habitat to the outer one. For **walk** to be applicable while staying within the limits, the two locations moved between have to be neighboring. The locations can also be of any type, either a *habitat* or a *location*. The effect would be that the *ranger* is no longer at the starting location, but instead at the destination location.
```
move

A ranger moves from one location to another. Requires the ranger to be at the starting location and for the locations to be neighboring. The ranger is then at the destination location.
```

Next, the ranger moves the deer from one habitat to another. In its more general form, this should let a *ranger* move any *animal*. This requires the *ranger* and the *animal* to be at the same habitat and for the destination habitat to be next to the starting habitat. The effect is that it sets both the *ranger* and *animal* to be at the new habitat. Since *animal*s can only be in *habitat*s, this action can only move between habitats.
```
move_animal

A ranger moves an animal from one habitat to another neighboring habitat. Requires the ranger to be in the habitat where the animal currently is and the target habitat to be neighboring. The animal and the ranger are then in the new habitat instead.
```

#### Use-case 2 actions
In use-case 2, the ranger starts by walking from an outer location to the center location and then from the center location to another outer location. The **walk** action is already defined above, and this new use-case doesn't need us to redefine it unless we want to change how it works. The current definition is good however, so no need to redefine it.

Then the ranger guards the outer habitat after walking to it. To be able to guard a *location* the *ranger* must be at the *location*. The ranger is then guarding the location.
```
guard

A ranger starts guarding a habitat. Requires the ranger to be at the habitat. The habitat is then marked as being guarded by the ranger.
```

However, since a *ranger* can only guard habitats where they are present they should not be able to move while guarding a location, otherwise they will still be set as guarding the destination they left. So, let's redefine the **move** and **move_animal** actions to address this additional precondition.
```
move

A ranger moves from one location to another. Requires the ranger to be at the starting location and for the locations to be neighboring. Additionally, the ranger must not be guarding the starting location. The ranger is then at the destination location.
```

```
move_animal

A ranger moves an animal from one habitat to another neighboring habitat. Requires the ranger to be in the habitat where the animal currently is and the target habitat to be neighboring. Additionally, the ranger must not be guarding the starting location. The animal and the ranger are then in the new habitat instead.
```

#### Use-case 3 actions
In use-case 3, the ranger stops guarding the habitat. In general, this should be applicable to any *location*. It will require that the *ranger* is at the *location* and currently guarding it. Afterwards, the *ranger* is no longer considered to guard the location.
```
unguard

A ranger stops guarding a habitat. Requires the ranger to be at the habitat and currently guarding it. The guard is then no longer considered to guard the location.
```

In the end of use-case 3, the ranger moves back to the parking lot. This uses the previously defined **walk** action and we don't need to redefine the current version of the action.

#### Use-case 3 actions
The construction worker Bob starts by building the roof. This will require that the worker is at the location, that there is an order, and that the roof isn't already built. It will remove the order, and create the roofs.
```
build_roof

A worker performs an order to build a roof at house. Requires the worker to be at the house, for a roof construction order to exist, for the walls to be built, and that no roof already exists. This uses up the roof construction order and creates the roof.
```

Finally, the house is finalized. For a house to be finalized, the roof and walls both have to be constructed. Furthermore, the worker must be at the location. The effect is that the house becomes finalized.
```
finalize_house

A worker finalizes construction on a house that already has walls and a roof. Requires the worker to be at the house, for a wall to be built, and for a roof to be built. This then marks the house as finalized.
```

#### Implicit actions
The following are the actions that were not used in the use-cases, but which still are necessary to model the domain.

The administrator lacks the ability to create a roof construction order. Creating a roof order requires an administrator and a target house. The house should not already have a roof or roof order. It leads to a wall order existing (which is later tracked with a predicate).
```
create_roof_order

An administrator creates an order to build the roof of a house. Requires that the roof has not already been built at the house. Requires that no order already exists. It creates a wall construction order.
```

## Example 2
### Domain and Problem
I've got a building with five floors and two elevators, and I want you to plan the elevator trips for Bob at floor 3 and Sally at floor 2 to both go to floor 5 in a climate-friendly way.

### Types
- object: Everything is an object
    - floor: Different levels in the building, numbered 1 to 5.
    - elevator: Elevators that can transport people between floors.
    - person: Individuals like Bob and Sally who need to travel to different floors.

floor is a/an object
elevator is a/an object
person is a/an object

### Limits
The following limits are mentioned or are implicit assumptions:
- Persons must be in an elevator or at a floor at all times.
- Elevators can only move between floors.
- Persons cannot move on their own; they must use an elevator to travel.

### Use-cases
#### Use-case 1
Bob enters the elevator on floor 3, travels to floor 5, and exits.
- The elevator at floor 3 picks up Bob -> **pick_up**
- Elevator drives to floor 5 -> **drive**
- Bob gets out of the elevator on floor 5 -> **drop_off**

#### Use-case 2
An elevator drives to floor 2, picks up Sally, goes to floor 5, and drops her off.
- An elevator drives to floor 2 -> **drive**
- Sally gets in the elevator at floor 2 -> **pick_up**
- The elevator drives to floor 5 -> **drive**
- Sally gets out of the elevator on floor 5 -> **drop_off**

### Actions
#### Use-case 1 actions
In the first use-case, Bob is picked up by the elevator. To perform this action, the *person* must be at the *elevator*'s floor. They are then considered to be inside the elevator rather than at the floor.
```
pick_up

An elevator picks up a person. Requires the person to be at the same *floor* as the elevator. The person is then considered to be inside the elevator instead of at the location.
```

Next, the elevator drives to floor 5. This requires the *elevator* to be at the starting *floor*. However, we assume that each *elevator* can reach any floor, so we don't need to consider connectivity.
```
drive

An elevator moves from one floor to another. Requires the elevator to be at the starting floor. After the action, the elevator is at the destination floor instead.
```

Finally, Bob exits the elevator at floor 5. To perform this action, the *person* exiting must be inside the *elevator*, and the *elevator* must be at the target *floor*.
```
drop_off

An elevator drops off a person at a floor. Requires that the person is inside the elevator and that the elevator is at the floor. The person is then no longer inside the elevator but instead at the floor.
```

#### Use-case 2 actions
In the second use-case, we use the **drive**, **pick_up**, and **drop_off** actions. These actions are all already defined, and the previous definitions are sufficent.

## Example 3
### Domain and Problem
The AI agent is here a scheduling system for a house construction company with administrators who can create orders for the construction of house parts, workers who perform them, and vehicles to transport the workers. Each house is modeled as having walls and a roof (which must be built after the walls). Once both of these are built, the house can be finalized by a worker, though this doesn't require any specific admin order.

Currently, there are a couple of trucks at the Chicago depot and we're trying to build three houses in the area. Jamie is the only administrator, but we've got Emma and Bob available for construction work.

### Types
- object: Everything is an object
    - worker: Human workers who do things
        - administrator: Workers who can create orders for other workers
        - construction_worker: Workers who can perform a variety of tasks, if there is an order for it
    - vehicle: Used to transport workers.
    - location: Places where workers and machines can be
        - house: What is constructed. Can be finalized when all components are built

worker is a/an object
administrator is a/an worker
construction_worker is a/an worker
vehicle is a/an object
location is a/an object
house is a/an location

### Limits
The following limits are mentioned or are implicit assumptions:
- Construction workers can only build a wall or a roof if given an order for it
- Walls have to be built before roofs
- Construction workers have to be at a location to perform work there
- Locations cannot move
- Workers and vehicles will always be at some location

### Use-cases
#### Use-case 1
One possible use-case would be for an administrator to order the construction of walls at a house, which is then constructed by a worker:
- Administrator Jamie orders the construction of walls at house 1 -> **create_wall_order**
- Construction worker Emma builds the walls at house 1 -> **build_wall**

#### Use-case 2
Another possible use-case would be the transportation of Bob from the Chicago depot to house 2 via truck:
- Bob enters the truck -> **enter_vehicle**
- The truck drives from Chicago depot to house 2 -> **drive_vehicle**
- Bob exits the truck -> **exit_vehicle**

#### Use-case 3
A third use-case is that Bob builds an already ordered roof, after which the house is finalized.
- Construction worker Bob builds the roof at house 2 -> **build_roof**
- Bob then finalizes the house -> **finalize_house**

### Actions
#### Use-case 1 actions
In use-case 1 the administrator creates a wall order for a specific house. For this action to be applicable, the house should not already have a wall or wall order. It leads to a wall order existing. Since we can't create new objects in PDDL the existence of orders is best modeled with separate predicates for each order.
```
create_wall_order

An administrator creates an order to build the wall at a house. Requires that the wall has not already been built at the house. Requires that no order already exists. The wall construction order is then created.
```

In use-case 1 we also see that the worker can perform the construction of walls. This will require that the worker is at the location, that there is an order, and that the wall isn't already built. It will remove the order, and create the walls.
```
build_wall

A worker performs an order to build a wall at a house. Requires the worker to be at the house for a wall construction order to exist and that no wall already exists. This consumes the wall order but creates a wall.
```

#### Use-case 2 actions
In use-case 2, Bob enters a vehicle and is driven to a location. Likely, any worker could do this. To enter a vehicle at a location, the worker and vehicle must be at that location. The end result is that the worker no longer is at the location, but instead inside the vehicle.
```
enter_vehicle

A worker enters a vehicle at a location. Requires that both the worker and vehicle are at the location. The worker is then no longer considered as being at the location. Instead, the worker is considered to be inside the vehicle.
```

Next, the truck drives from one location to another. To move between two locations the vehicle has to be at the first location. As no connectivity is mentioned, we assume that all locations are directly connected.
```
drive_vehicle

A vehicle moves from one location to another. The vehicle is at the starting location. Afterwards, the vehicle is no longer at the starting location, but instead at the second location.
```

Finally, Bob exits the truck. To exit a vehicle at a location, the worker must be inside the vehicle and the vehicle must be at that location. The result is that the worker is no longer in the vehicle, but instead at the location.
```
exit_vehicle

A worker exits a vehicle at a location. Requires that the worker is inside the vehicle and that the vehicle is at the location. The worker is no longer inside the vehicle. Instead, the worker is at the location.
```
